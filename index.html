DOME LTDome LightOverhead lighting in the cockpit that provides general illumination for the flight deck, essential for night operations and low-light conditions. It ensures proper visibility of controls and instruments without causing glare on the windscreen.
PANEL LTPanel LightAdjustable lighting system that illuminates the instrument panel, allowing pilots to read gauges and controls in low-light conditions without affecting night vision. Typically features dimming controls for optimal visibility.
CABIN LTCabin LightControls the main passenger cabin lighting system, including reading lights, overhead lighting, and emergency lighting. Includes dimming capabilities and night lighting modes to maintain a comfortable cabin environment during all phases of flight.
ENG FIREEngine FireWarning system that detects and alerts the crew to engine fires. Includes fire detection loops, warning lights, and fire suppression systems. When activated, it triggers fire extinguishing procedures and may automatically shut down affected systems.
APU FIREAPU FireDedicated fire detection and suppression system for the Auxiliary Power Unit. Monitors for fire conditions in the APU compartment and provides visual and aural warnings. Includes fire extinguishing capability that can be activated from the flight deck.
FIRE TESTFire TestAllows crew to test the functionality of fire detection and warning systems. When activated, it checks the integrity of fire detection loops, warning lights, and aural alerts. A required part of pre-flight checks to ensure all fire protection systems are operational.
CARGO FIRECargo Fire DetectionMonitors cargo compartments for fire or smoke using smoke detectors and heat sensors. Provides early warning of potential fire hazards in inaccessible areas. May be connected to fire suppression systems to control fires without crew intervention.
DISCHDischarge IndicatorIndicates when a fire extinguishing agent has been discharged into a designated area. Shows the status of fire suppression systems and helps crew monitor the effectiveness of firefighting actions. May show pressure or chemical release confirmation.
APAutopilotAutomated flight control system that can maintain aircraft attitude, altitude, heading, and speed without constant pilot input. Can follow pre-programmed flight plans and includes multiple modes for different phases of flight. Reduces pilot workload during long flights.
FDFlight DirectorFlight instrument that provides visual guidance to the pilot, showing the required pitch and roll inputs to follow a specific flight path. Works with navigation systems to guide the aircraft along the intended route, approach path, or holding pattern.
YDYaw DamperAutomatic system that detects and counteracts yawing oscillations (Dutch roll) using the rudder. Improves passenger comfort and reduces structural stress on the airframe. Particularly important in swept-wing aircraft that are prone to Dutch roll tendencies.
TO/GATakeoff / Go-AroundButton or switch that commands maximum available thrust for takeoff or go-around procedures. When activated, it sets the engines to a pre-determined thrust setting and configures flight guidance systems for the appropriate phase of flight.
HDGHeadingFlight control mode that maintains the aircraft's current magnetic heading. When engaged, the autopilot will adjust the aircraft's bank angle to maintain the selected heading, compensating for wind drift. Essential for VFR navigation and ATC vectoring.
ALTAltitude HoldAutopilot mode that maintains the aircraft at a specific barometric altitude. The system automatically adjusts pitch and power to hold the selected altitude, compensating for atmospheric changes and weight variations. Can be overridden by the pilot for altitude changes.
V/SVertical SpeedFlight control mode that maintains a specific rate of climb or descent in feet per minute. The pilot selects the desired vertical speed, and the autopilot adjusts the aircraft's pitch to maintain that rate until reaching a new altitude or the mode is changed.
LNAVLateral NavigationAutopilot mode that follows the lateral (left/right) path of the active flight plan. The aircraft will automatically follow the programmed route, making turns at waypoints and following airways or approach procedures as defined in the FMS.
VNAVVertical NavigationAdvanced autopilot mode that manages the aircraft's vertical flight path according to the flight plan. Controls altitude, airspeed, and thrust to meet altitude restrictions at waypoints, optimize fuel efficiency, and follow standard departure/arrival procedures.
SPD / MACHSpeed / MachAutopilot mode that maintains either indicated airspeed (SPD) or Mach number (MACH). At lower altitudes, airspeed is typically used, while at higher altitudes, Mach number becomes the standard reference. The system automatically switches between them based on altitude or pilot selection.
FLCHFlight Level ChangeAutopilot mode that changes altitude while maintaining a specific airspeed or Mach number. The system adjusts thrust to maintain the target speed while the aircraft climbs or descends. Ideal for efficient altitude changes during cruise or when cleared to a new flight level by ATC.
TRIMTrimSystem that adjusts the neutral position of flight control surfaces to reduce control forces and maintain desired attitude without constant control input. Includes elevator, aileron, and rudder trim. Can be manually adjusted or automatically controlled by the autopilot.
FLAPFlapHigh-lift devices on the wings that increase lift and drag when extended. Used during takeoff and landing to allow slower approach speeds and steeper descent angles. Different flap settings (degrees of extension) are used for various phases of flight and performance requirements.
SPOILERSpeed BrakeAerodynamic surfaces that can be deployed to increase drag and reduce lift. Used to control airspeed during descent, increase descent rate without gaining excessive speed, and assist with braking after touchdown. Some aircraft combine spoilers and speed brakes into a single system.

NAV 1/2Navigation RadioDual VHF navigation receivers that tune and receive signals from VOR, VOR/DME, ILS, and localizer transmitters. Each radio can be independently tuned to different navigation aids, allowing for cross-checking of position and approaches. Displays course deviation and glideslope information.
COMM 1/2Communication RadioDual VHF communication transceivers for air traffic control communications. Allows monitoring of two frequencies simultaneously and quick switching between frequencies. Includes controls for frequency selection, volume, and squelch. Essential for maintaining contact with ATC and other aircraft.
ADFAutomatic Direction FinderRadio navigation instrument that indicates the direction to a non-directional beacon (NDB) ground transmitter. Displays the relative bearing to the station and can be used for navigation, holding, and non-precision approaches. Being phased out in favor of GPS in many regions.
XPDRTransponderRadio transceiver that responds to secondary surveillance radar (SSR) interrogations from ATC. Transmits a four-digit squawk code and, in Mode C/S, the aircraft's pressure altitude. Essential for air traffic control radar identification and separation. Required in controlled airspace.
TCASTraffic Collision Avoidance SystemOnboard system that monitors nearby aircraft equipped with transponders. Issues traffic advisories (TA) and resolution advisories (RA) to help pilots avoid mid-air collisions. TCAS II provides vertical resolution commands (climb/descend) when a collision threat is detected.
GPWSGround Proximity Warning SystemSystem that alerts pilots if the aircraft is in immediate danger of flying into the ground or an obstacle. Uses radio altimeter, GPS, and terrain databases to provide early warnings. Includes modes for excessive descent rate, terrain closure, altitude loss after takeoff, and unsafe terrain clearance.
WX RADARWeather RadarForward-looking radar system that detects precipitation intensity and storm cells. Displays weather patterns in color-coded formats to help pilots navigate around hazardous weather. Modern systems can also detect wind shear and turbulence. Essential for flight planning and in-flight weather avoidance.
FMSFlight Management SystemIntegrated avionics system that automates navigation, performance, and flight planning. Combines data from multiple sensors to determine position, calculate optimal routes, and manage fuel efficiency. Interfaces with autopilot and flight director systems. Central to modern glass cockpits.
CDUControl Display UnitInterface unit for the Flight Management System, typically featuring a keyboard and display screen. Allows pilots to enter and modify flight plans, access navigation databases, monitor system status, and program performance parameters. The primary means of interacting with the FMS.
IRSInertial Reference SystemNavigation system that uses laser gyroscopes and accelerometers to calculate the aircraft's position, attitude, and velocity without external references. Requires alignment before flight and provides highly accurate short-term position data. Often integrated with GPS for improved accuracy.
GPSGlobal Positioning SystemSatellite-based navigation system that provides precise position, velocity, and time information. Used for en-route navigation, terminal procedures, and precision approaches. Modern systems include WAAS/EGNOS for improved accuracy and integrity, enabling LPV approaches with vertical guidance.
SEAT BELTSSeat Belts SignIlluminated sign in the cabin that indicates when passengers should remain seated with seat belts fastened. Controlled from the flight deck and typically activated during taxi, takeoff, landing, and when expecting turbulence. Part of the aircraft's safety systems.
NO SMOKINGNo Smoking SignIlluminated sign indicating that smoking is prohibited. Required to be on during all phases of flight on most commercial aircraft. Part of the aircraft's fire prevention system and a regulatory requirement. Often combined with the seat belt sign controls.
EMER EXIT LTEmergency Exit LightsIllumination system that marks emergency exits and escape paths in the cabin. Automatically activates during emergencies or power failures using battery backup. Includes floor-level lighting to guide passengers to exits in smoke-filled conditions. Tested during pre-flight checks.
EVAC CMDEvacuation CommandSwitch or button that activates the aircraft's emergency evacuation system. When activated, it typically arms emergency slides, activates emergency lighting, and may sound an alarm. Used only when immediate evacuation is necessary and should be confirmed by both pilots.
OXYOxygen SystemProvides supplemental oxygen to crew and passengers during cabin depressurization or smoke/fumes events. Includes crew masks with microphones, passenger drop-down masks, and portable oxygen bottles. Crew masks typically provide 100% oxygen on demand or pressure demand.
LDG GEARLanding Gear LeverControl handle that raises and lowers the aircraft's landing gear. Typically has three positions: UP, OFF, and DOWN. Includes safety features like squat switches and gear warning systems. The lever is often shaped like a wheel to prevent confusion with other controls.
AUTO BRKAuto BrakeSystem that automatically applies the brakes after landing or during a rejected takeoff. Can be set to different deceleration rates. Reduces pilot workload during landing and ensures consistent braking. Automatically disengages when reverse thrust is selected or manual braking is applied.
ANTISKIDAnti-SkidSystem that prevents wheel lockup during braking by modulating brake pressure. Improves braking efficiency and directional control, especially on wet or slippery runways. Works in conjunction with the auto-brake system and is active during all ground operations.
PARK BRKParking BrakeSystem that applies pressure to the main landing gear brakes to prevent aircraft movement when parked. Should be set whenever the aircraft is parked and engines are shut down. Also used during engine start to prevent aircraft movement. Must be released before taxi.
BRAKE TEMPBrake TemperatureMonitoring system that displays the temperature of the wheel brakes. High brake temperatures can indicate excessive braking and may require a cooling period before the next takeoff. Critical for flight safety as overheated brakes can cause tire explosions or brake fires.
ONPower OnPosition on a switch that activates a system or component. When selected, power is supplied to the associated system. Should only be activated when required for operation and when all preconditions for system activation are met. Part of standard checklists.
OFFPower OffPosition on a switch that deactivates a system or component. Stops power supply to the associated system. Used during normal shutdown procedures, in emergencies, or when a system is not required. Some critical systems may have protective features that prevent accidental shutdown.
ARMArm / ArmedSwitch position that prepares a system for automatic activation when certain conditions are met. For example, arming the speed brake system allows automatic deployment when reverse thrust is selected after landing. The armed state is typically indicated by an amber light.
DISARMDisarmAction to deactivate or prevent automatic system activation. Used when automatic system operation is not desired or could be hazardous. For example, disarming the auto-spoiler system prevents automatic spoiler deployment after landing. Should be done in accordance with checklists and procedures.
PUSHPush ButtonMomentary switch that activates a function when pressed. May be backlit or labeled for identification. Used for actions like testing systems, acknowledging warnings, or activating time-limited functions. Some push buttons have protective covers to prevent accidental activation.
PULLPull HandleManually operated control that requires pulling to activate critical or emergency systems. Often has a protective cover and may be brightly colored (typically red or yellow). Used for fire extinguishers, emergency fuel shutoff, or other safety-critical functions. Requires deliberate action to operate.
TESTTestFunction that verifies the proper operation of a system or component. When activated, the system performs a self-check of its components and indicators. Used during pre-flight checks and maintenance. Successful test is typically indicated by specific light patterns or display messages.
RESETResetButton or switch that restores a system to its initial state or clears a fault indication. May be used to recover from system errors or warnings after the underlying issue has been addressed. Should only be used in accordance with approved procedures and checklists.
FAULTFault IndicatorWarning light or message that indicates a system malfunction or abnormal condition. Typically illuminated in amber or red, depending on severity. May be accompanied by an aural warning. Requires crew attention and may necessitate specific procedures outlined in the Quick Reference Handbook (QRH).
